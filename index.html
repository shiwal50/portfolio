JetSend — Transactional Messaging
Maropost · CRM & Marketing Automation · 2024
● Shipped & Live
My Role Discovery Initiator · Requirements Contributor · Cross-functional Coordinator
Teams Involved Product · Engineering · QA · Customer Success
Timeline Zero to GA in under 6 weeks
Hypothesis
Enterprise clients were abandoning transactional workflows not because of user error, but because the platform had no native reliability layer — forcing dangerous workarounds that silently failed at scale.
How I Found It — Before Any PM Involved
  • Noticed a recurring ticket pattern: 3–5 enterprise clients per week reporting failed transactional sends — each escalated as a one-off, never surfaced as systemic
  • Ran my own analysis across ticket history. Found the same three failure modes every time: no delivery status visibility, no retry logic, no failure alerting — product gaps, not config errors
  • Client behaviour evidence: multiple accounts had built manual workarounds (spreadsheet-tracked resend queues) — indicating the problem was critical enough to absorb significant operational cost
  • I brought this to the PM team — it was not a task I was given. I initiated the conversation with the evidence already structured
Discovery Process I Ran With the PM
  • Conducted structured client interviews alongside the PM — gathering pain points, workaround behaviour, and willingness-to-switch signals
  • Created a detailed JTBD document with supporting references and case evidence — this became the requirements foundation
  • Enterprise marketing ops teams needed guaranteed delivery with audit trails for compliance — platform offered neither
  • Developer-integrated customers relied on webhook-triggered sends for order confirmations — silent failures had direct business impact downstream
Trade-offs Considered (MECE)
  • Option A — Documentation fix only: Rejected. The failure was architectural, not instructional. Documentation cannot add retry logic.
  • Option B — Per-client workaround support: Rejected. Does not scale, creates tech debt, rewards the loudest customer not the right product decision.
  • Option C — Native transactional layer (JetSend) ✓ Selected: Addresses root cause, scales, closes the competitive gap. Three capability pillars: delivery reliability, visibility, and failure alerting.

Honest Reflection — What I'd Do Differently
I did not own a formal post-launch metrics review. I know ticket volume dropped in the targeted categories from qualitative signal and team feedback — but I did not establish a rigorous before/after baseline. In future work, I will define success metrics upfront — a specific, time-boxed number agreed with PM before launch, not after.

RouCN — D2C Sneaker Brand · India
Independent PM Case Study · Founder · GTM Lead · 2025–Ongoing
● Live PM Case Study — Pre-Launch
ⓘ This is an independent PM case study built to demonstrate discovery-to-roadmap thinking across 7 documented sections. No inventory has been produced. No manufacturer is finalised. All research, personas, and roadmap are the PM deliverable — not operational outputs.
Budget (planned) ₹10,00,000
Launch Target August 2026 · D2C first
Case Study Status 7 sections documented · Ideation phase
Hypothesis
Indian Gen Z sneaker buyers in the ₹2,500–4,500 price band are underserved not on price, but on identity. The first Indian D2C brand to crack narrative + quality in this band will earn disproportionate loyalty. RouCN's raccoon positioning — scrappy, adaptive, nocturnal, everywhere — is ownable at this price point.
Market Signal & Secondary Research
  • India footwear market: $20.67B in 2025, growing at 9.7–12.9% CAGR through 2033 (IMARC Group)
  • Competitive gap: Nike/Adidas enter at ₹7,000+; local brands (Sparx, Campus) compete on price with no identity — no brand owns the mid-market with a story
  • Comet benchmark: scaled to 12,000 orders/month on story-driven drops alone, starting with a single hero product before expanding
  • BIS QCO 2024 compliance becomes a structural moat against informal sellers who cannot list on regulated marketplaces
Planned Primary Research (not yet conducted)
  • 15–20 qualitative interviews across 4 persona segments — planned to begin Q3 2026
  • 200+ response survey on price sensitivity and brand triggers
  • Size-fit observational research (Indian feet are typically wider — a key product spec input)
4 Customer Personas (secondary research-derived)
  • The Urban Hustler (22–30, Metro): Nike aesthetics, resents ₹9K price tax · Price ceiling ₹3,500–4,500
  • The College Signal-Sender (18–23, Tier 1–2): brand logo as social currency · Price ceiling ₹2,500–3,500
  • The Tier 2 Aspirant (20–28, Lucknow/Jaipur/Indore): largest volume opportunity · Price ceiling ₹2,500–4,000
  • The Conscious Spender (25–35, any tier): post-pandemic made-in-India convert · Highest LTV potential
