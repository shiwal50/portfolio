JetSend — Transactional Messaging
Maropost · CRM & Marketing Automation · 2024
Shipped & Live
My RoleDiscovery Initiator · Requirements Contributor · Cross-functional Coordinator
Teams InvolvedProduct · Engineering · QA · Customer Success
TimelineZero to GA in under 6 weeks
Hypothesis
Enterprise clients were abandoning transactional workflows not because of user error, but because the platform had no native reliability layer — forcing dangerous workarounds that silently failed at scale.
How I Found It — Before Any PM Was Involved
  • Noticed a recurring ticket pattern: 3–5 enterprise clients per week reporting failed transactional sends — each escalated as a one-off, never surfaced as systemic
  • Ran my own analysis across ticket history. Found the same three failure modes every time: no delivery status visibility, no retry logic, no failure alerting — product gaps, not config errors
  • Client behaviour evidence: multiple accounts had built manual workarounds (spreadsheet-tracked resend queues) — the problem was critical enough to absorb significant operational cost
  • I brought this to the PM team — it was not a task I was given. I initiated the conversation with the evidence already structured
Discovery Process I Ran With the PM
  • Conducted structured client interviews alongside the PM — gathering pain points, workaround behaviour, and willingness-to-switch signals
  • Created a detailed JTBD document with supporting references — this became the requirements foundation
  • Enterprise marketing ops teams needed guaranteed delivery with audit trails for compliance — platform offered neither
Trade-offs Considered (MECE)
  • Option A — Documentation fix only: Rejected. The failure was architectural, not instructional.
  • Option B — Per-client workaround support: Rejected. Does not scale, creates tech debt.
  • Option C — Native transactional layer (JetSend) ✓ Selected: Addresses root cause, scales, closes the competitive gap.

HighLevel CRM — Product Teardown
Independent Product Thinking · 2026
Product Thinking
Format9-section editorial HTML teardown
Basis10 months of daily enterprise-level use
StatusPublished live
What I Did
After 10 months embedded in HighLevel's ecosystem daily, I structured my observations into a 9-section product teardown covering: core value prop, user segmentation, UX friction points, automation builder limitations, AI capabilities, competitive gaps, and three specific prioritised improvements I would ship as PM.
Key Findings From the Teardown
  • Automation builder: Works well for linear flows. Breaks down at conditional branching with 2+ outcome paths — no native error-state visibility at the branch level. Failures surface through client complaints, not platform signals.
  • White-label positioning: A genuine moat — but the agency onboarding flow assumes too much technical literacy from non-technical white-label clients, creating early churn risk
  • AI features: Promising but siloed. The AI agent, conversation AI, and workflow AI don't share context — leading to redundant configuration effort for clients who want end-to-end automation
  • Three changes I'd prioritise: (1) branch-level error visibility in the automation builder, (2) a unified AI context layer across the platform's AI features, (3) a simplified non-technical onboarding track for white-label client sub-accounts

Maropost Marketing Cloud — Product Teardown
Independent Product Thinking · 11 months inside the platform · 2024
Product Thinking
Format9-section editorial HTML teardown · B2B SaaS · Email + Ecommerce Automation
Basis11 months as Client Support Specialist · cross-referenced with G2 Winter 2025 reports
StatusPublished live · shiwal50.github.io/portfolio/maropost-teardown.html
What I Did
After 11 months embedded in Maropost daily — supporting enterprise clients like Mercedes-Benz Canada, BioTrust Nutrition, and REDEF Group — I structured my product observations into a 9-section teardown covering architecture, user segments, journey friction, feature depth, competitive positioning, and three prioritised PM recommendations.
Key Findings From the Teardown
  • eRFM AI scoring is the real moat: No other platform at this price tier combines Recency, Frequency, Monetary, and Email engagement data into automatic audience scoring without manual tagging — this is a genuine differentiator that Klaviyo charges significantly more for
  • Onboarding is the biggest churn risk: The platform's power is real — but the path to that power requires upfront technical configuration (custom field mapping, domain auth, journey logic) that non-technical mid-market teams consistently stumble on. Support tickets from this friction dominated my queue.
  • Agency use case is underbuilt: Agencies managing multiple brand accounts are using the platform for multi-client management but there is no unified agency dashboard — they're working around this with individual logins, which creates unnecessary operational overhead and churn risk
  • Three changes I'd prioritise as PM: (1) smart field-matching on CSV import to reduce onboarding friction, (2) a formal agency dashboard with cross-account reporting, (3) transactional messaging natively within the platform — which is exactly the gap I later addressed with JetSend
Why This Matters for My PM Story
  • The JetSend initiative I led at Maropost was a direct output of this analytical mindset — I was pattern-matching across client pain points and surfacing them as structured product inputs before I ever wrote a formal teardown
  • This teardown is the documented version of how I think: from user behaviour evidence to hypothesis to prioritised recommendation — the same loop I apply to every product problem
