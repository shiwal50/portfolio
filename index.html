JetSend — Transactional Messaging
MAROPOST · CRM & MARKETING AUTOMATION · 2024
Shipped & Live
My Role Discovery Lead · Requirements Owner · Cross-functional Coordinator
Teams Involved Product · Engineering · QA · Customer Success
Timeline Zero to GA in under 6 weeks
Hypothesis
Enterprise clients were abandoning transactional workflows not because of user error, but because the platform had no native reliability layer — forcing dangerous workarounds that silently failed at scale.
Signal & Evidence
  • Recurring ticket pattern: 3–5 enterprise clients per week reporting failed transactional sends — each treated as a one-off, never surfaced as a systemic product gap
  • Root cause analysis revealed the same three failure modes across all cases: no delivery status visibility, no retry logic, and no failure alerting — product gaps, not configuration errors
  • Client behaviour evidence: multiple accounts had built manual workarounds (spreadsheet-tracked resend queues), indicating the problem was critical enough to absorb significant operational cost
  • Competitive gap: at least two competing platforms offered native transactional reliability features at this tier — creating a retention risk for Maropost's enterprise segment
Discovery — Who Was Affected and How
  • Enterprise marketing ops teams: needed guaranteed delivery with audit trails for compliance and SLA reporting — existing platform offered neither
  • Developer-integrated customers: relied on webhook-triggered transactional sends for order confirmations and system alerts — any silent failure had downstream business impact
  • Support & CS teams (internal): spending disproportionate time on repeat delivery issues that required Engineering escalation every time — no self-serve resolution path
Trade-offs Considered (MECE)
  • Option A — Documentation fix only: Rejected. The failure was architectural, not instructional. Documentation can't add retry logic.
  • Option B — Per-client workaround support: Rejected. Doesn't scale, creates tech debt, and rewards the loudest customer not the right product decision.
  • Option C — Native transactional layer (JetSend) ✓ Selected: Addresses root cause, scales, and closes the competitive gap. Three capability pillars: delivery reliability, visibility, and failure alerting.

ROUCN — D2C Sneaker Brand · India
FOUNDER · PRODUCT MANAGER · GTM LEAD · 2025–ONGOING
Live · Pre-launch
Budget ₹10,00,000
Launch Target August 2026 · D2C first, Myntra Month 3
Metrics Note Projected targets based on market research & comparable D2C launches. Actuals updated post-launch.
Hypothesis
Indian Gen Z sneaker buyers in the ₹2,500–4,500 price band are underserved not on price, but on identity — they want a brand with a story, not just a shoe with a logo. The first Indian D2C brand to crack narrative + quality in this band, pre-Myntra, will earn disproportionate loyalty.
Market Signal & Evidence
  • Market: ₹4.6L Cr Indian footwear market growing at 12% CAGR; 62% of buyers aged 18–35 now purchase online first
  • Competitive gap: Nike/Adidas enter at ₹4,000+; local brands (Sparx, Campus) compete on price with no identity play — zero middle-ground with a brand story
  • Consumer interviews: 14 conversations across Lucknow, Jaipur, and Delhi revealed consistent pattern — buyers stretching ₹3,000+ for 'something that means something'
  • Myntra category data: sneakers in ₹1,999–4,999 range see highest cart abandonment, indicating demand without satisfactory options
Customer Segmentation — 4 Personas
  • The Urban Hustler (22–30): Nike aesthetics, resents ₹9K price tax, buys on Myntra during sales — micro-influencer-led purchase decisions
  • The College Signal-Sender (18–23): brand logo as social currency, will stretch ₹3,000 for the right story, Instagram-first discovery path
  • The Tier 2 Aspirant (20–28): Lucknow / Jaipur / Indore · most underserved segment, fastest-growing, heavy Amazon/Flipkart buyer
  • The Conscious Spender (25–35): post-pandemic pivot to Indian brands; highest LTV potential if first purchase delights — natural brand advocate
Trade-offs Considered
  • Multi-SKU launch (5 silhouettes): Rejected. Spreads budget across unvalidated designs; inventory risk before any sell-through data. Classic feature bloat applied to D2C.
  • Marketplace-first (Myntra Day 1): Rejected. No brand equity before listing = just another SKU in a filter. D2C-first forces brand story to earn the customer.
  • Premium positioning (₹5,500+): Rejected. Research didn't support WTP above ₹4,500 for a zero-recognition brand. Price credibility must be earned.
  • One hero silhouette, three colourways, D2C-first at ₹3,499 ✓ Selected: Minimum viable product decision. Validate, then expand.

What I'd Do Differently — In Advance
I'd have started the manufacturing partnership conversations 2 months earlier. The BIS certification timeline compressed the pre-launch window significantly. In hindsight, the product decision (what to build) was faster than the operational decision (who builds it). That sequencing matters in D2C.
